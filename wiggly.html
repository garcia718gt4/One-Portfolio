Wiggly

Project

Under the UX Techdegree program from Treehouse, I completed a case study to explore the needs of shoppers who use technology to shop for groceries.

Audience

The target audience was people who use a mobile phone when shopping online at least once a week.

Goal

The goal of the study was to design a task flow for a grocery shopping app. The project was conducted between January and February of 2021.

Responsibilities

Designing an app from conception to delivery: conducting interviews, paper and digital wireframing, low and high-fidelity prototyping, conducting usability studies, accounting for accessibility, and iterating.

EMPATHIZE

Survey Research Method

Participants were recruited for a quick survey study about their habits from a local grocery store on two different days and times. Moreover, each participant was asked five questions along five domains including: 1. Thoughts and feelings about using an app to shop, 2. Prepardness before shopping, 3. Past issues, 4. Experience during shopping, and 5. Issues when shopping.

Insights

Participants enjoy a fast or convenient shopping experience and like finding on-sale items easily. In addition, most participants enjoyed the ease of ordering groceries online but do not like high paying high delivery fees. A couple emerging pain points from the survey included if the person forgets an item they intended to purchase and when they miss a sale.

IDEATE

Wireframes

After gathering user survey data, I designed a task flow (for a usability study in the next section) on wireframes for the grocery store app to correct for users’ pain points. For example, I thought about displaying a ribbon on the item to show when items are on sale. Lastly, I included a feature at the checkout screen to remind the user if they have all the items they need, if not, they can go back and continue shopping.

TESTING / RESULTS

Usability Study

A total of five adults were recruited and tested individually. Specifically, the researcher instructed participants to complete a task flow of a new grocery store app. The task instructions required participants to navigate through the app to locate the number of items in their cart and view their total before placing their order. All participants used the same laptop and reported their thoughts and feelings after the study.

Results

1. Most participants reported the app was easy to use and looked clean and simple

2. A couple participants did not understand the small closing x button after first glance.

SUMMARY

The case study required this author to design a task flow for a new grocery store app based on feedback from real shoppers’ thoughts, feelings, and pain points. As shown, I believe the hi-fidelity prototype succeeded in accomplishing the goals for this case study. That is, a user is currently able to select a main item, navigate a subcategory, view their cart and make changes if needed, proceed to the checkout screen, and finally, view their list of items to double check if they have everything they need. Overall, the author designed a prototype with users’ experiences of grocery shopping in mind. However, the researcher had freedom to explore the user interface.

For example, the researcher was influenced by the famous midwestern grocery store, “Piggly Wiggly,” when designing the final prototype. Thus, the colors red, white, and shades of gray were used throughout the design. Moreover, the author used Fredoka One, a close resembling typeface to that of the famous grocery store, to display the main headers and labels. (Museo Sans Rounded 1000/900 are used on this web page, which better match the “Piggly Wiggly” typeface.) Lastly, to bring history to modern technology, the author named the app, “Wiggly.”

LEARNINGS

This author was a little nervous going to a grocery store and asking people if they had time to participate in a brief survey study. However, this part of the case study was perhaps the most valuable part of the design process because it allowed for in-depth understanding of users, their habits, and design choices. For example, it was helpful to learn most participants have forgotten an item from the store that they intended to purchase before arriving at the store. This knowledge helped the author commit to better design choices.
